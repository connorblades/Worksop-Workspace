# Local Ranking Playbook. GBP, Reviews, Citations

**Worksop Workspace** · Marketing · *Off-site execution plan*

Status: Draft v1 · Last updated: 17 May 2026

Pairs with `seo-keyword-research.md`, `seo-gap-analysis.md`, `seo-action-plan.md`. The on-site SEO/AEO work is done (Phases 1-5). This playbook covers the off-site work that actually moves Worksop Workspace into the local Map Pack and onto the first page for "coworking Worksop" and adjacent queries.

---

## Why this matters more than more pages

For a brand-new domain in a small local market, on-page SEO has diminishing returns past a certain point. The real first-page lever in Worksop is the Google Map Pack (the three businesses with a pin shown above the blue-link results). Ranking in the Map Pack depends on three things, in this order:

1. **Google Business Profile (GBP)** completeness and activity.
2. **Google reviews**: count, recency, and the keywords reviewers use.
3. **Citations and backlinks**: consistent NAP across UK directories and inbound links from local sites.

The website tells Google *what* you are. GBP, reviews and citations tell Google *that you exist and that real people care*.

Do this in order. Don't skip GBP for citations. Don't chase backlinks until reviews are flowing.

## Part 1. Google Business Profile (GBP)

### 1.1. Claim and verify

If not already done:

1. Go to https://www.google.com/business and sign in with the account you want to own the listing long-term (use a Google Workspace account on the worksopworkspace.com domain if possible, not a personal Gmail).
2. Search for "Worksop Workspace" — if a listing already exists from a third party, claim it. If not, create it.
3. Verify by postcard (Google posts a code to 30 Carlton Road; takes ~5 working days). Sometimes Google offers phone or video verification — accept whichever they offer.
4. Once verified, add the second owner (whoever runs marketing) as a manager so there's a backup if you lose access.

**Critical:** the address, phone and name on GBP must match exactly what's on the website footer and every directory. Inconsistent NAP is a known ranking suppressor. Cross-check after every update.

### 1.2. Optimise the profile

Fill in every field, even the optional ones. Each filled field is a small ranking signal.

| Field | What to put |
|---|---|
| Business name | `Worksop Workspace` (not "Worksop Workspace Ltd", not "Worksop Workspace Coworking" — keyword-stuffing the name violates Google's guidelines and risks suspension) |
| Primary category | `Coworking space` |
| Secondary categories | `Serviced office`, `Office space rental agency`, `Meeting room rental`, `Conference center` (Google's preset list, pick the closest 3-5) |
| Address | `30 Carlton Road, Worksop, S80 1PH` |
| Service area | Leave **blank**. We have a physical address customers visit. Adding a service area on a storefront listing can dilute rankings. |
| Hours | Mon-Fri 8am-5pm. Update "special hours" for bank holidays as they come (Christmas, Easter, May bank holidays). |
| Phone | `07739 063734` (must match website footer) |
| Website | `https://worksopworkspace.com` |
| Appointment / booking link | Add the membership/waiting list URL: `https://worksopworkspace.com/membership` |
| Short description (750 char) | "Worksop Workspace is the first modern coworking space in Worksop town centre. Hot desks from £12 per day or £30 per week, dedicated desks £50 per week (Phase 2), private offices from £79 per week all-inclusive, a professional meeting room at £20 per hour and a recording studio at £40 per hour (Phase 3). Free parking, 900 Mbps fibre, free tea and coffee. Bus stop outside, 8 minutes' walk from Worksop railway station. Used by remote workers, freelancers, tradespeople, parents, therapists and small business owners across Worksop, Retford, Tickhill, Bawtry, Maltby, Dinnington, Harworth, Clowne and Bassetlaw." |
| Attributes | Tick every relevant attribute: free Wi-Fi, free parking, wheelchair accessible (if true), gender-neutral toilets, identifies as women-owned (if applicable), credit cards, contactless payment, etc. |
| Services list | Add each priced product as a service line: Day Pass £12, Hot Desk Weekly £30, Dedicated Desk Weekly £50, Private Office from £79/week, Meeting Room £20/hour, Studio £40/hour. Each service can have its own description. |

### 1.3. Photos

GBP rewards listings with regular photo uploads. Aim for **20+ photos** at launch, then add 2-4 new ones each month.

- **Logo and cover photo:** brand-consistent, high res
- **Building exterior:** the Carlton Road entrance from across the road, at street level, in daylight
- **Reception / entrance:** the first thing a member sees walking in
- **The open coworking floor:** taken at a busy time (with permission), so it looks alive
- **A hot desk and a dedicated desk:** show what each product actually looks like
- **A private office:** door open, lit, monitor on the desk
- **The meeting room:** screen on, chairs around the table
- **The studio:** mics, headphones, acoustic treatment
- **The kitchen / coffee area:** the cultural shot, what people actually do at break time
- **Parking:** confirm free parking with a photo, not just words
- **Team and members:** with their permission, faces help the listing convert

Geotag every photo with the Carlton Road coordinates (latitude 53.3038, longitude -1.1237) before upload. Most phones do this automatically when location services are on.

File names matter (a little). Don't upload `IMG_3942.jpg`. Rename to `worksop-workspace-hot-desk.jpg`, `worksop-workspace-private-office.jpg`, etc.

### 1.4. Google Posts

Treat GBP like a mini social channel. Post **at least weekly**.

Use the three GBP post types:

1. **Update** posts (general news, blog post promos, behind-the-scenes)
2. **Offer** posts (founding member rates, limited launch pricing) — these display a CTA button on the listing
3. **Event** posts (open day, launch event, member meetup)

Each post should:
- Be 100-300 words
- Include one image
- Link to a relevant page on worksopworkspace.com (NOT the homepage every time — vary the destination)
- Have a clear CTA button

**Cadence target:** one post per week minimum, ideally two. Posts expire after 7 days for updates, but the engagement counts toward the listing's activity signal.

### 1.5. Q&A

Pre-seed the Q&A section. You can post questions yourself and answer them as the business. Pre-seed the top 8-10 questions:

1. How much does a day pass cost?
2. Do I need to be a member to use the meeting room?
3. Is there free parking?
4. What broadband speed do you have?
5. Can I bring my child?
6. Is the studio open yet?
7. What's the closest train station?
8. Do you offer virtual office services?
9. What are your opening hours?
10. Can I take a phone call from a hot desk?

Pre-seeded answers give real visitors confidence without needing to ask. Keep each answer short (1-2 sentences) and include a link to the relevant page.

## Part 2. Reviews

### 2.1. Why this is the highest-leverage thing

Three businesses sit in the Worksop Map Pack at any moment. The one with the most recent, highest-rated, keyword-rich reviews will tend to win the click. Reviews also feed Google's AI-generated summaries and ChatGPT/Perplexity citations.

**Target:** 25 Google reviews within 90 days of opening. 50 within 6 months. Maintain a 4.7+ star average.

### 2.2. The ask

Most members will leave a review if asked correctly. Most won't if not asked at all.

**When to ask:**
- After their first full week as a member (the "I love this place" honeymoon window)
- After a positive interaction (compliment, fixed problem, used the studio for the first time)
- After their first month, with a thank-you note

**How to ask, in person:**
> "Hey, can I ask a favour? If Worksop Workspace has been useful for you, a Google review really helps us be found by people in your situation. Two minutes. Here's the link." (then hand them a card with a QR code)

**How to ask, by email:**
- Trigger 8 days after first payment (first week complete)
- One short email, no attachments, one CTA button
- Subject: "A two-minute favour"
- Body: "Hi [name], if Worksop Workspace has been useful this week, a Google review helps people in similar situations find us. It takes about two minutes. [Leave a review →] Thanks. [your name]"

**How to ask, by SMS (optional):**
- Trigger 14 days after first payment, only if no review yet
- One short text with the short link

### 2.3. The link

Get the Google review short link from your GBP dashboard ("Get more reviews" → copy link). It will look like `https://g.page/r/...`. Use that exact URL everywhere, never a generic Google search URL.

Print it as a QR code on:
- The waiting-list confirmation page
- A small card by the coffee machine
- The receipt/welcome email
- A laminated card at reception

### 2.4. Responding to reviews

Respond to **every** review within 48 hours. Yes, every one. Positive and negative.

**Positive review response template:**
> "Thanks [first name]. Glad [specific thing they mentioned] worked for you. See you next week. [your name]"

Keep it short, mention the specific thing they liked (signals to Google that the review is about the actual business), use the member's first name.

**Negative review response template:**
> "Hi [first name]. Sorry to hear that. We try to get [the thing] right and it sounds like we missed on this. Can I call you to make it right? hello@worksopworkspace.com. [your name]"

Never argue, never deny, never blame the customer. Acknowledge, apologise, offer to take it offline. Other prospects will read every negative review and your response. The response is what matters.

### 2.5. The keywords reviews use

Reviewers tend to repeat the language they see on your site. By using the phrases "coworking in Worksop", "private office in Worksop", "meeting room in Worksop" on the site and in your in-person language, you nudge reviewers towards those phrases. Reviews with the local keyword in the body are stronger ranking signals than reviews without.

**Don't ever ask** a member to include a keyword in their review. That violates Google's guidelines and risks the review being filtered. Just make the language familiar through exposure.

### 2.6. Review platform priorities

In this order of priority:

1. **Google reviews** (single biggest impact, on the GBP listing) — primary focus
2. **Facebook recommendations** — secondary, but easy
3. **Trustpilot** — only if you've decided to invest in it; otherwise skip
4. **Sector-specific:** Coworker.com lists UK coworking spaces and has its own review system. Worth claiming the listing.
5. **Industry directories:** OfficeRiders.com, FlexOffices, etc. — claim, but reviews are low-volume

## Part 3. Citations and local directories

### 3.1. What a citation is

A citation is any place on the web that lists the business name, address and phone (NAP) — even without a link. Consistent citations across the major UK directories tell Google that the business is real, established, and trusted.

**NAP consistency rule:** the exact same business name, the exact same address (down to the punctuation and the order of fields), the exact same phone number, on every listing. If the website says "30 Carlton Road, Worksop, S80 1PH" with no comma, every directory should match. Picky but real.

### 3.2. The starter citation list

Submit to these, in this order. Most are free, a few are paid.

**Tier 1 — must do (free, high authority):**

| Directory | Notes |
|---|---|
| Google Business Profile | (covered in Part 1) |
| Bing Places for Business | Microsoft's equivalent. Free. Underrated because Bing also feeds Edge, Duck and ChatGPT search. |
| Apple Business Connect | Free. Feeds Apple Maps, Siri, Spotlight on every iPhone/Mac. Underrated for iOS-heavy demographics. |
| Yell.com | UK's biggest directory. Free basic listing. |
| Yelp UK | Free listing. Often used by ChatGPT and Perplexity for citations. |
| FreeIndex | UK SME directory, free listing. |
| Thomson Local | Free listing. |
| Scoot | Aggregates to multiple smaller UK directories. |
| 192.com | Business listings. |
| Cylex UK | Free listing. |

**Tier 2 — sector-specific (free, often easy wins):**

| Directory | Why |
|---|---|
| Coworker.com | UK's main coworking directory. Has its own review system. |
| FlexOffices | Office and coworking aggregator. |
| OfficeRiders | Meeting room and event space directory. |
| Hubble | Coworking and serviced office aggregator. |
| Office Genie | UK office search. |
| Liquidspace | Has UK presence. |

**Tier 3 — local Bassetlaw (free):**

| Directory | Why |
|---|---|
| Bassetlaw District Council business directory | Council often runs a local business register. |
| Visit Bassetlaw / Visit Notts | If running events open to the public. |
| Worksop Chamber of Commerce | Membership may be required. |
| FSB (Federation of Small Businesses) | Paid membership. Gives a member directory listing. |
| North Notts BID | Worth checking what their member directory looks like. |

**Tier 4 — review aggregators and AI feeders:**

| Directory | Why |
|---|---|
| Glassdoor (employer listing) | Only if you plan to grow staff |
| LinkedIn Company Page | Free. Critical for AI assistants pulling business data. |
| Crunchbase | Free if your business profile is genuine. |
| Wikipedia (no) | Worksop Workspace doesn't qualify yet under notability guidelines. Don't try to game it. |

### 3.3. Submission process

For each Tier 1 and Tier 2 directory:

1. Search the directory for "Worksop Workspace" first. If a listing exists (often auto-generated from public data), **claim it** rather than create a duplicate.
2. If creating new, use the canonical NAP. Copy-paste from a single source-of-truth document so they all match.
3. Use the same short description (500-750 chars) across directories. Slight variation is fine for the parts directories let you customise; the core facts should be identical.
4. Add the same primary photo across directories where they accept photos.
5. Link out to the appropriate page on worksopworkspace.com. Mix it up: half should link to the homepage, half to /membership, a few to /meeting-room.
6. Track in a spreadsheet (suggested columns: directory name, URL, listing URL, submission date, status, login email).

**Time budget:** plan for 3-4 hours of submissions across one or two evenings. After the initial round, citations need only periodic maintenance (annual review for accuracy).

### 3.4. NAP audit

Two months after the citation push:

1. Search Google for `"Worksop Workspace"` (with the quotes).
2. Cross-check the first three pages of results. Any listing with the wrong address, phone or business name? Click in, update or claim it, correct.
3. Search Google for `"30 Carlton Road" Worksop`. Catch any orphan listings that name the address but not the business.

## Part 4. Backlinks (local press, partners, sponsors)

### 4.1. Why local backlinks matter

A single link from worksopguardian.co.uk or bassetlawvoice.co.uk is worth more than 100 links from generic directory pages. Local relevance + topical relevance is what Google rewards.

### 4.2. The low-hanging fruit

**Founding sponsors:**

- Bullseye Properties (https://www.bullseyeproperties.co.uk/) — request a "we sponsor Worksop Workspace" page or a mention on their about/community page, linking back.
- South Yorkshire Property Buyers (https://southyorkshirepropertybuyers.com/) — same ask.

Both have a relationship; both should be willing. Specific ask: a sentence on their site, linked, ideally in the body of an existing page (not just the footer).

**Local press:**

| Outlet | Pitch angle |
|---|---|
| Worksop Guardian | "First modern coworking space opens in Worksop town centre" — straight local-business launch story |
| Mansfield Chad | Same story, broader North Notts angle |
| Bassetlaw FM | Radio interview. Drive-time slot is realistic if you can speak about local working life |
| Lincolnshire Live / Derbyshire Times | A1 corridor / cross-border worker angle |
| BBC Radio Nottingham | Local business slot |
| North Notts BID newsletter | Member-spotlight inclusion if you join |

**Pitch one outlet at a time**, not a press release blast. Personalise the email. Lead with the local-economy hook (a new business in Worksop town centre, council-area employment, working-from-home patterns post-pandemic), not with us.

**Sample pitch email:**

> Subject: Coworking on Carlton Road. quick story idea
>
> Hi [name],
>
> A new coworking space is opening on Carlton Road in Worksop. small story, but maybe relevant given how many people in Bassetlaw are working from home or commuting to Sheffield.
>
> We've put together a fair comparison of what's actually available in Worksop for flexible workspace (us, Worksop Turbine, Middletons Yard, Enterprise Business Centre). Happy to share it.
>
> If you'd like an interview or a building visit, I'm Connor at hello@worksopworkspace.com. No pressure either way.
>
> Connor Blades
> Worksop Workspace
> worksopworkspace.com

### 4.3. Partnership backlinks

For each partner relationship, ask for a link as part of the partnership.

| Partner type | Ask |
|---|---|
| Local accountants / solicitors / IFAs we recommend | A "recommended workspace" link on their resources page, in exchange for us linking to them |
| Local trade body or chamber | Member directory inclusion (already in citations list) |
| Local schools / education (if you do a parents partnership) | A "recommended for school-holiday working" mention |
| Local cafés / restaurants (members welcome) | A "near coworking space" mention, in exchange for our "where to grab lunch nearby" recommendations |

Each partner link is small. Twenty small partner links is significant.

### 4.4. Content that earns links

Two types of content tend to attract links without asking:

1. **Original data.** If we ever survey our members about working patterns ("X% of Bassetlaw remote workers want a closer coworking option"), local press will link to it for free.
2. **Local-interest comparisons.** The `/blog/coworking-options-in-worksop` post is exactly this shape. Send it to local press as a reference; they may quote and link.

### 4.5. Things NOT to do

- **Don't buy links.** Manual penalty risk. Stay away from "100 backlinks for £50" services.
- **Don't reciprocate links indiscriminately.** Three reciprocal links with closely related local businesses are fine. Three hundred is a pattern Google detects.
- **Don't write guest posts on irrelevant sites.** A guest post on a coworking industry blog is fine. A guest post on a generic SEO blog about coworking is a waste of time and looks artificial.
- **Don't stuff the anchor text with keywords.** "Worksop Workspace" or "the Carlton Road coworking space" or a natural URL is what local links should look like. "Best coworking space Worksop" as anchor text on every link is a spam signal.

### 5.2. Quarterly review

Every quarter:

1. Re-run a citation NAP audit (see Part 3.4).
2. Re-run the AEO test queries (ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Gemini, Google AI Overview): "what coworking spaces are in Worksop", "how much does coworking cost in Worksop", "private office Worksop", "meeting room hire Worksop", "where to work during school holidays in Worksop".
3. Check Google Search Console for any new high-impression / low-CTR keywords. Improve those page titles and meta descriptions.
4. Pull one piece of original member-data insight for a blog post.

## Part 6. What this playbook does NOT cover

- **Paid Google Ads / Local Services Ads** — separate decision, separate budget. Probably not worth running until organic GBP is bedded in (otherwise hard to measure incremental).
- **Paid social** — separate decision, separate playbook.
- **Influencer / partnerships** — covered loosely in Part 4 but no detailed playbook.
- **Email marketing** — separate. Use Mailchimp/GHL for member newsletters; not an SEO lever directly.
