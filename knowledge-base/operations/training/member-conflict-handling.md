# Member Conflict Handling

**Worksop Workspace** · Training · *Phase 1 reference*

Status: Draft v1 — needs Connor review · Last updated: 14 May 2026

---

How to handle situations where something's not right — a member's behaviour is affecting other members, two members have a problem with each other, or someone's pushing the house rules. Goal: protect the space, protect the experience, keep your composure.

The house rules are in `../../members/house-rules.md`. This doc is about enforcing them without making a meal of it.

---

## The brand position (read this first)

Worksop Workspace is a calm space. People come here to work, not to deal with drama. Whatever the situation, the way you handle it should leave members feeling that the space is well-run and that staff have things under control.

A few principles:

- **Quiet, not silent.** The space isn't a library. A bit of conversation, a kettle going, someone making a coffee — all fine. The bar isn't "no noise"; it's "no noise that pulls focus from someone else's work".
- **One bad actor can ruin it for everyone.** That's why we move firmly when someone is consistently disrupting other members. Soft on people, firm on the rules.
- **Never publicly shame.** Always a quiet word, away from other members.
- **No drama at the door.** If something escalates, take it to a private office or step outside. The hot desk area never sees a fight.

---

## The four-step framework

Every conflict situation follows the same shape:

1. **Listen** — to whoever raised it (or whoever's involved). Don't interrupt. Don't take sides yet.
2. **Acknowledge** — repeat back what you heard so the person knows you understood. "So just to make sure I've got this — Mark was on a call without headphones and it was making it impossible for you to focus."
3. **Propose** — offer the next step. "I'll have a quiet word with him now. Either he wears headphones or he moves the call. Either way it stops."
4. **Escalate** — only if step 3 doesn't land. (See "When to escalate" below.)

This works for almost every situation.

## Common scenarios

### Loud phone call in the quiet zone

What's happening: someone's taking a long or loud call in a hot desk area.

What to do:

- Walk over calmly. Don't hover. Just stand at a reasonable distance until they notice.
- "Mind if I have a quick word when you're done?"
- When they're off the call: "We've got quiet zones here — would you mind taking your next call in a private office or the kitchen area? Just for the focus of the people around you."
- 95% of people apologise instantly and move. Most genuinely didn't realise.

If they push back: "Of course you can stay at your desk for quick calls — anything longer than a minute or two, that's when we ask people to move."

If they keep doing it: that's now a pattern, not a one-off. Escalate.

---

### A member soliciting / pitching other members

What's happening: someone is approaching other members trying to sell to them, get them on a call, hand out business cards.

This is the **single biggest red line** in the brand. Worksop Workspace is not a networking event. From `members/house-rules.md`:

> Members who use the space to solicit other members for business will not be welcomed back.

What to do:

- Wait until they're back at their desk, away from the person they were pitching
- Have a quiet, private word: "Hey, can I grab you for a sec? Just a heads-up — the space here isn't really a networking hub. People come here to work and they're not really up for being pitched at the coffee machine. Just so you know how it works."
- Most people back off immediately when they realise the vibe.
- If they argue or do it again, that's a strike. Two strikes and the membership ends. Log every incident in `../../members/feedback-log.md`.

---

### A member complaining about another member

What's happening: Member A comes to you to complain about Member B.

What to do:

- Listen properly. Don't dismiss it.
- Acknowledge: "Sounds frustrating — let me deal with it."
- Don't tell Member A what you're going to say to Member B. Just say you'll handle it.
- Go and have the quiet word with Member B using the framework above.
- Come back to Member A within 30 minutes and say it's been handled. Don't go into detail about what was said.

**Never** force the two members to confront each other. We mediate; we don't referee.

---

### A guest behaving inappropriately

What's happening: a member's day-guest is behaving in a way that's not OK — phone on speaker, taking up multiple desks, loud, etc.

What to do:

- Approach the member, not the guest. The member is your relationship.
- "Quick one — would you mind asking [name] to move to a private office for that call?"
- The member usually handles it from there.
- If repeated, the same rules apply: the host member is responsible for their guest.

---

### Dispute over a booking

What's happening: someone thinks they booked the boardroom / podcast room (Phase 3) at a time another member shows up for.

What to do:

- Open the member app, check the booking log. The app is the source of truth.
- Whoever's booking is in the log gets the room.
- For the displaced party: apologise, offer an alternative slot, offer a credit if appropriate.
- Never let a dispute become a confrontation in front of other members.

---

### Aggressive or threatening behaviour

What's happening: someone is raising their voice, becoming abusive, or making others feel unsafe.

This is a different category — not a house-rules conversation, a safety issue.

What to do immediately:

- Get other members to safety. Stand between the aggressive person and other members if needed.
- Don't engage with anger. Speak slowly, low volume, hands visible: "I need you to step outside please."
- If they refuse or escalate further: call 999 (police). Don't try to be a hero.
- Document everything in detail afterwards.
- That membership is over. No second chances for genuine threats.

Cross-reference `../sops/sop-010-emergency-evacuation.md` for the "other emergencies" section.

---

### Complaint about the space itself

What's happening: a member is frustrated about something we control — Wi-Fi slow, coffee machine broken, room too cold, etc.

What to do:

- Take it seriously, even if it feels small. To them, in that moment, it's not small.
- "Sorry about that — let me fix it now."
- Fix what you can immediately. Log what you can't fix today in `../sops/` or `../../../07-facilities/maintenance-log.md` (Drive).
- Follow up with them within the same day to tell them what happened next.

A complaint handled well is worth more than no complaint at all — that's a member who'll stay.

## When to ask someone to leave

The bar is high but not infinite. Ask someone to leave (and end their membership) for:

- Repeated soliciting or networking pressure after a warning
- Aggressive or threatening behaviour (one strike, no warning needed)
- Persistent disregard for quiet zones after multiple conversations
- Damage to the space, equipment, or another member's property
- Anything illegal
- Anything that creates an unsafe environment for staff or other members

How to do it:

- Always in private — private office or outside, never on the open floor
- Calm tone. "I'm sorry, this isn't working out. We're going to need to end your membership."
- Don't argue or justify at length. Say it once. Don't be drawn into a debate.
- Process the cancellation through the member app the same day
- Send a follow-up email confirming what happened, briefly and factually

## How to document an incident

For anything beyond a routine "had a word with someone about phone calls":

In `../../members/feedback-log.md` (or a dedicated incident log if/when we add one), record:

1. **Date and time**
2. **Members / visitors involved** (names, day-pass or member, plan if member)
3. **What happened** — factual, no opinion. What you saw or were told.
4. **What you did** — every step you took
5. **Outcome** — was it resolved? Membership status?
6. **Whether escalated** — to Connor, to emergency services, to anyone else

This isn't paperwork for paperwork's sake. If a pattern emerges (same member, same problem, escalating), the log is what tells us.

## What to remember when it's stressful

- Take a breath before you respond. Two seconds of pause is invisible to them; it changes everything for you.
- You don't have to win the argument. You have to protect the space.
- It's OK to say "let me come back to you in 5 minutes" if you need a moment to think.
- You can always escalate. That's not a failure; that's the system working.
- Document everything that's not routine. Future-you will thank present-you.
