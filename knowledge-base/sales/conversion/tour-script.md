# Tour Script

**Worksop Workspace** · Training · *Phase 1 reference*

Status: Draft v1 — needs Connor review · Last updated: 14 May 2026

---

The actual words to say when you're walking a new member or prospective member through the space. Companion to `../sops/sop-001-opening-the-space.md` and `../sops/sop-003-member-onboarding-tour.md` — the SOP is what you do, this is what you say.

The tour should feel like showing a friend round, not a sales pitch. Don't read this verbatim. Use it as the spine, then sound like yourself.

---

## Before they arrive

If a tour is pre-booked:

- Glance at their enquiry note in GHL or the member app — what did they say they're looking for?
- Make sure the coffee machine is on and the place looks tidy
- Have a spare welcome pack ready on reception

If they walk in cold:

- Don't make them wait. Drop what you're doing. Eye contact, smile, "Welcome in — first time here?"

---

## 1. The greeting (60 seconds)

> "Welcome to Worksop Workspace. I'm [name]. Have you been before, or is this your first time?"

If it's their first time:

> "Brilliant. Let me show you round. Can I make you a coffee first or shall we walk and talk?"

Why this works: lowers the temperature instantly. They came in expecting to be sold to; you've offered a coffee instead.

---

## 2. The discovery (3–4 minutes)

While you're making their drink — or sitting them down with one — ask, in this order:

> "So what do you do for work?"

Listen. Note: are they an employee, self-employed, running a small team?

> "Where are you working from at the moment?"

Listen. Common answers: home, café, van, a friend's office.

> "What's not working about that?"

This is the magic question. Their answer tells you exactly which plan to lead with later. Common signals:

- "Kids at home" → hot desk or weekly pass; the quiet zone matters
- "Calls from the van" → weekly pass or private office if they need privacy regularly
- "Spending a fortune in coffee shops" → weekly pass — show them the maths
- "Trying to look professional for clients" → private office or meeting room (Phase 3)
- "Just want somewhere I'm not at home" → day pass first, let the space do the selling

> "And roughly how often do you reckon you'd use a space like this?"

Frequency tells you which plan suits. Daily → weekly pass or dedicated desk. A few days a week → weekly pass. Occasional → day pass to start.

**You don't have to ask every question.** If they're already telling you the answer, don't interrogate. Match their energy.

---

## 3. The route

Walk in this order. Keep moving — a tour that drags is a tour that loses them.

### Stop 1: Reception and lockers

> "This is reception — where you'll come in each day. Lockers are over here, they're free, you can grab one whenever you arrive."

Point. Don't open every locker.

### Stop 2: Hot desk area

> "This is where most people start. Any seat is yours for the day. People plug in wherever feels right — by a window, near the coffee, in a corner."

Walk through, don't stand and lecture. Note any members at desks — give them a smile, don't interrupt.

### Stop 3: Dedicated desk area

> "These are dedicated desks. If you're in most days, you can have one of these — same seat, every day, you leave your monitor up if you want. It's £[price] a week and works out cheaper than the weekly pass if you're here four days or more."

Why this works: you've explained the upgrade trigger without selling it.

### Stop 4: Private offices

> "Private offices through here — for people who want a lockable door. Used by small teams of two, three, four. Starts at £79 a week. We've got [n] of them; have a peek through the glass."

If empty, walk in briefly. If occupied, keep walking.

### Stop 5: Refreshment area

> "Coffee, tea, water — all included. No buying anything to justify your seat. Help yourself. The machine takes about thirty seconds to make a flat white if you ever want one."

This is the moment that lands the brand. Most people have spent £4 in a coffee shop today already.

### Stop 6: Quiet vs social zones

> "Ground floor is the quiet zone — headphones on, heads down. If you fancy some background energy there'll be a basement area opening later this year with music and a more coffee-shop feel. Same membership, just a different vibe depending on what you need that day."

### Stop 7: Toilets and fire exits

> "Toilets through there. Fire exits are signposted — quick walkthrough if there's ever an alarm: out the front, or the back through there, both go to the assembly point in the car park."

Casual mention. Don't make it feel formal. But say it.

### Stop 8: Parking

> "Parking's outside, free, no time limit — which in Worksop town centre is the rare bit."

Smile when you say it. Locals appreciate the joke.

---

## 4. The maths moment (optional)

If they said "café" or "home" in discovery, do the maths out loud:

> "Just so you've got the numbers — day pass £12, weekly pass £30. So if you're here three days a week, the weekly pass is cheaper than three day passes. If you're here every day, you're looking at £6 a day — about the cost of a coffee and a sandwich."

Don't push. Just plant the seed.

---

## 5. The app and Wi-Fi

Back at reception:

> "Two things I'll set you up with — the Wi-Fi and the member app. Wi-Fi password is on the card here, you can scan in for the day, scan out when you leave. Takes a second. The app is also how you'd book anything — desks, meeting room, podcast room — later down the line."

Help them download the app. Show their QR code.

---

## 6. The settle-in

If they're committing today:

> "Want me to set you up on the weekly pass? Stripe handles the payment, comes out automatically, you cancel any time via the app. No contract, no notice."

Walk them through Stripe checkout. Confirm the first payment.

If they want to think about it:

> "Totally fine. Day pass £12 if you fancy trying a day first — no commitment. Or take the welcome pack with you and have a think. I'll be here if you've got questions."

**Never push. Never use urgency.** The pricing is fixed and they can come back any time. That's the whole pitch.

---

## 7. Settle them at a desk (if they're staying)

Walk them to a desk that fits what they said they wanted. Quiet if they mentioned focus, near the action if they want energy.

> "Make yourself at home. Coffee's all yours, I'm around if you need anything."

Step away. Let them work.

---

## 8. Before they leave (any first visit)

> "How was it? Anything you'd change?"

This starts the feedback loop. Log anything noteworthy in `../../members/feedback-log.md`.

---

## Things to never say

- "Act now / limited time / don't miss out" — see `../../brand-guidelines.md` words-to-avoid list
- "Are you a networker?" — wrong audience
- "Our competitors all..." — punching at the locals looks petty
- "Have you got a card?" — we don't trade cards; that's the opposite of the brand
- Anything that sounds like a sales script. If a phrase feels rehearsed, swap it for plain language.

---

## Things to always do

- Use their name once you have it
- Match their energy — chatty if they're chatty, brief if they're brief
- Treat them like an adult who already knows what they need
- Make sure they see one member already working when you tour the hot desk area (social proof without saying a word)
- End every tour with a question, not a pitch
